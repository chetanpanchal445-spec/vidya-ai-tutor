Chapter 8: Transport in Plants — Osmosis and Diffusion

8.1 Diffusion
Diffusion is the movement of molecules from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration. This movement continues until the concentration becomes equal on both sides — a state called dynamic equilibrium. Diffusion is a passive process, meaning it does not require any energy (ATP).

Examples of diffusion: When you spray perfume in one corner of a room, the smell eventually spreads throughout the room. This is diffusion of perfume molecules in air.

In plants, carbon dioxide (CO2) enters leaves through tiny pores called stomata by diffusion. Oxygen produced during photosynthesis also exits through stomata by diffusion.

8.2 Osmosis
Osmosis is a special type of diffusion. It is the movement of water molecules from a region of higher water concentration (dilute solution) to a region of lower water concentration (concentrated solution) through a semi-permeable membrane.

A semi-permeable membrane is a membrane that allows only certain molecules to pass through it — in this case, water molecules can pass but dissolved solutes (like salt or sugar) cannot.

The pressure that develops due to osmosis is called osmotic pressure. The more concentrated the solution, the higher its osmotic pressure.

8.3 Types of Solutions
Hypotonic Solution: A solution that has lower solute concentration than the cell. When a cell is placed in a hypotonic solution, water enters the cell by osmosis. The cell swells up. In plant cells, this is called a turgid state — the cell wall prevents bursting. Turgor pressure helps plants stay upright.

Hypertonic Solution: A solution that has higher solute concentration than the cell. When a cell is placed in a hypertonic solution, water leaves the cell. The cell shrinks. In plant cells, this is called plasmolysis — the cell membrane pulls away from the cell wall. This is why plants wilt when overwatered with salt water.

Isotonic Solution: A solution that has equal solute concentration as the cell. No net movement of water occurs. Cells maintain their normal shape.

8.4 Importance of Osmosis in Plants
Water absorption by roots: Root hair cells have higher solute concentration than the soil water. So water enters root hair cells by osmosis. This is how plants absorb water from the soil.

Cell to cell movement: Water moves from cell to cell by osmosis, from cells with higher water concentration to cells with lower water concentration — this creates a gradient that helps move water upward through the plant.

Stomatal opening: Guard cells become turgid (fill with water by osmosis) to open the stomata. When they lose water, they become flaccid and stomata close.

8.5 Plasmolysis
When a plant cell is kept in a hypertonic solution, it loses water. The protoplasm (living content) shrinks and pulls away from the cell wall. This is called plasmolysis. A plasmolysed cell appears to have a gap between the cell membrane and the cell wall. This process is reversible — when placed back in water, the cell regains its turgidity through osmosis (deplasmolysis).
